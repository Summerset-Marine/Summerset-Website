Great Lakes Waterfront Construction: Door County

Door County's waterfront is unlike anything else in Wisconsin. Whether your property sits on Green Bay, along Sturgeon Bay, or on the open Lake Michigan shoreline, you're dealing with conditions that simply don't exist on inland lakes. Wave heights, ice pressure, bottom variability, and the sheer scale of Great Lakes water movement require a different level of engineering — and a contractor with real Great Lakes experience.

The Scale of Great Lakes Conditions

On an inland lake, a significant wave event might produce 18 to 24 inches of wave height. On Green Bay's open water, northerly winds regularly produce waves of 4 to 6 feet, and storm events can push well beyond that. Lake Michigan's Door County shoreline is exposed to even greater fetch and open-water energy. Structures that perform perfectly on Okauchee Lake or Geneva Lake would fail quickly under these conditions. Every Door County installation requires site-specific engineering that accounts for the actual wave climate at that location.

Ice Pressure on Great Lakes Shores

Ice pressure on Great Lakes shorelines is in a different category from inland lake ice. Wind-driven ice sheets can exert thousands of pounds of force per linear foot on waterfront structures. Any system built on Door County's Great Lakes shoreline must be engineered to either resist this force or allow ice to pass without damage. Summerset Marine's engineering team designs specifically for the ice regime at each site — there is no generic solution for Great Lakes ice pressure.
