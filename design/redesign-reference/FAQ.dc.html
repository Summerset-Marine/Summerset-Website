Pier Systems — 8 Questions
A permanent pier is engineered to remain in the water year-round, including through Wisconsin's freeze-thaw cycles. It uses driven posts set deep into the lake bottom, anchored against lateral ice movement. A seasonal dock is installed each spring and removed each fall — it's not designed to withstand ice loads and typically requires annual maintenance and replacement on a shorter cycle. Most lakefront owners find that the long-term cost of seasonal docks, including installation labor, storage, and replacement, exceeds the investment in a quality permanent system within 8–12 years.
System selection depends on your water depth, bottom composition, wave exposure, and desired aesthetic. The All Seasons HD is our most robust system, suited for open-water exposures and depths up to 14 feet. The All Seasons handles most standard Wisconsin lake conditions. The Classic is ideal for sheltered coves and moderate depths. The Minimalist prioritizes a clean visual profile at a lower investment point. We assess all of these factors during your site consultation before recommending a system — we never spec a product without seeing your specific waterfront.
Our pier systems are designed with an indefinite service life. The hot-dip galvanized steel framework is resistant to corrosion in fresh water, and our welded connections are not subject to the fatigue failures common in bolted seasonal systems. Decking materials have their own service lives — Ipe hardwood typically lasts 25–40 years; composite decking carries a manufacturer warranty of 25+ years. We have pier systems installed in the early 2000s still in active service today. This is backed by our lifetime structural warranty.
We offer Ipe hardwood (our most popular — beautiful, extremely durable, ages to a silver-gray), premium composite (low maintenance, consistent appearance, available in multiple colors), and cedar (a classic, cost-effective natural option). Each has different maintenance requirements and price points. We can show you samples at your consultation and discuss what's best for your usage, budget, and aesthetic preferences.

Boat Lifts — 5 Questions
A built-in lift is integrated directly into your pier's structural framework — the lift cradle lowers into an opening in the dock deck. This creates a cleaner aesthetic and uses deck space more efficiently. A standalone lift is a freestanding structure placed alongside a pier. Standalone lifts are easier to add to an existing pier and can sometimes be repositioned, but take up more lateral space. Both configurations handle the same vessel weights and sizes — the choice is primarily aesthetic and spatial.
Our lift systems are available in capacities from 2,500 lbs (suitable for most runabouts and pontoons up to 22 feet) through 10,000 lbs for larger cruisers and sport boats. We'll specify the right lift based on your vessel's current weight plus a safe margin — always bring your vessel's spec sheet to the consultation. We do not recommend undersizing a lift; the consequences of overloading are significant.
Yes. We offer single and double PWC lift configurations, both built-in and standalone. PWC lifts can be integrated into the side of a main pier or installed on a dedicated finger pier. Many clients pair a full boat lift with one or two PWC lifts in a single installation — we'll design the dock layout to accommodate all of your watercraft efficiently.
All of our standard lifts are hand-operated via a crank wheel — they're simple, reliable, and require no electrical connections. We can specify motorized (electric) lifts for clients who prefer push-button operation, subject to your local electrical code and DNR approval. Motorized lifts require a permitted electrical connection to the dock — our team coordinates this with your electrician.
Our built-in lift systems are designed to winter in place alongside our permanent piers. Standalone lifts can typically remain in place as well, though we recommend raising them to their highest position before freeze-up and confirming they're clear of ice formation zones. Our service team performs a fall winterization check on all lifts installed by Summerset as part of our residential service offering.
Installation — 5 Questions
Most residential pier installations take 1–3 days on-site, depending on pier length, water depth, and bottom conditions. Complex configurations — multi-slip layouts, long commercial piers, or difficult bottom composition — may require additional time. We provide a specific installation schedule with your project proposal. Our crews are experienced and efficient; most lakefront neighbors are surprised by how quickly the work is completed.
We install from ice-out in early spring through late fall. Spring is our busiest season — clients who want to be in the water by Memorial Day should submit their consultation request by January at the latest. We also install during fall and winter for clients planning ahead for the following season. Some post-setting work can be done through ice, though this requires specialized equipment and is scheduled case-by-case.
You don't need to be on-site for installation — we work from the approved plans and your pre-installation walkthrough. Most clients stop by at the end of the first day and for the final walkthrough, but the crew works independently in between. We document the installation with photos and provide a completion report. We do require access to the shoreline and any utilities per the plan — we'll confirm access details in advance.
Wisconsin lakes present a wide range of bottom conditions — sand, gravel, rock, soft silt, clay, and mixed profiles. We assess bottom composition during the site survey using sounding and probing equipment. Hard rock bottoms require core drilling and epoxy anchoring, which adds cost but provides exceptional holding strength. Soft silt bottoms require longer posts driven past the soft layer to firm substrate. We've encountered and solved every bottom condition Wisconsin lakes present — there is no situation we haven't handled.
We work from the water wherever possible to minimize shoreline impact. Most post-driving is done from a barge or work boat. For work that requires shore access, we use equipment sized to minimize turf damage and restore the area upon completion. We ask clients to mark any underground utilities, irrigation lines, or lighting conduits before our arrival — this is critical for avoiding damage to expensive shoreline improvements.
Permits & Regulations — 4 Questions
Most permanent piers in Wisconsin require a DNR pier permit. The specific type — standard pier permit vs. full Water Use Permit — depends on pier length, water depth, shoreline classification, and proximity to navigational channels or sensitive habitat. In addition, many municipalities and lake districts have their own setback and design requirements layered on top of DNR rules. Summerset's permit team handles the complete application process, including any municipal filings required for your specific parcel.
Standard pier permits typically process in 4–8 weeks from submission. Full Water Use Permits — required for larger or more complex structures — can take 3–6 months, sometimes longer if public comment periods are triggered. We submit applications as early as possible in the project timeline and follow up actively with the DNR. Most projects submitted in fall or early winter are permitted in time for spring installation.
Building without a required permit exposes you to significant risk: the DNR can require removal at your expense, impose fines, and in some cases issue stop-work orders that affect other property improvements. Unpermitted structures also create title issues that complicate property sales. We never begin construction without all required permits in hand — it protects you and it protects our warranty. If you've inherited an unpermitted structure, we can help assess the retroactive permitting options for your situation.
We handle permitting for all lakes within our seven service markets: Lake Geneva, Oconomowoc, Madison, Whitewater, Green Lake, Fox Chain, and Door County. We have established working relationships with the DNR regional offices covering these areas, as well as with the local lake districts and municipalities that issue supplemental approvals. If your lake is outside these markets, contact us — we may be able to assist or refer you to a qualified contractor in your area.
Warranty & Service — 4 Questions
Our lifetime structural warranty covers the galvanized steel frame, welded connections, post anchoring, and all structural components against defects in materials and workmanship for the life of the original installation. It does not cover decking (which is a wear surface with its own rated life), hardware (bolts, cleats, lifts), or damage caused by events outside our control including vessel collision, vandalism, or extraordinary natural events beyond normal Wisconsin lake conditions. The warranty is transferable to a subsequent property owner with written notification.

Pricing & Process — 4 Questions
Permanent pier pricing depends on length, width, system type, decking selection, water depth, and site conditions. As a general range, residential permanent pier projects on Wisconsin lakes run from approximately $25,000 for a straightforward shorter installation to $150,000+ for longer, deeper, or more complex configurations with multiple lifts. We don't publish price lists because every project is genuinely site-specific — your proposal will include a detailed line-item breakdown. We encourage you to request a consultation rather than making decisions based on general ranges.
After your inquiry, one of our lake-specific specialists contacts you to schedule a site visit — typically within a week. The visit itself takes 45–60 minutes: we assess your shoreline, take depth readings, review existing structures, and discuss your goals, budget range, and timeline. We then prepare a written proposal within five business days covering recommended system, configuration, permit requirements, estimated timeline, and investment. The consultation is completely no-obligation.
Yes. Upon acceptance of a proposal, we require a deposit to initiate permit applications and reserve your installation slot. Deposit amount and payment schedule are outlined in each proposal — typically 30% at signing, 40% at permit approval, and 30% upon installation completion. We accept check, ACH transfer, and major credit cards. Payment terms are firm; we do not begin fabrication or permitting without the initial deposit on hand.
Spring installation slots fill quickly — typically by February for a Memorial Day completion. Fall and the following spring are generally more available, but we advise beginning the conversation as early as possible regardless of your target date. If you have a specific completion deadline (a property closing, an event, a seasonal milestone), tell us at inquiry — we'll be straightforward about whether we can meet it. We do not overbook our crews; we'd rather decline a project than miss a committed date.
